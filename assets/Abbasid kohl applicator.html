Abbasid kohl applicator
9th century
Murwab, Qatar
"It is from 628 EC that the population of Qatar joined the Muslim community and belongs to the new province comprising the territories of Bahrain, Al-Hasa Oasis and Qatar. During the 9th century, Qatar took part in this immense Abbasid Empire and many traces allow us to evaluate a flourishing trade. Murwab, the largest settlement of this period is constituted of forty houses, one fort and two mosques. This kohl applicator stick made of bronze and copper alloy is a thin cylinder with two protrusions in its center that frames a rectangular parallelepiped. This object is related to cosmetics and was widely diffused in the Islamic world during the early Abbasid period. The main cosmetic product used by men, women and children for the eyes was kohl - not only is kohl a decorative cosmetic but also an ophthalmic medicine. This black mixture has been used since pre-Islamic times and the best varieties came from Iran. Its main substance was antimony sulphide powder. The mixtures contained lead among other metallic or mineral substances, and sometimes organic materials such as walnut shells and even soot were used. Some were made from a variety of substances, producing dark blue, dark red, purple or even yellow shades. These recipes existed alongside countless local and individual recipes, passed down through family tradition and health professionals. "

Abbassid Period
It is from 628 EC that the population of Qatar joined the Muslim community and belongs to the new province comprising the territories of Bahrain, Al-Hasa Oasis and Qatar. During the 9th century, Qatar took part in this immense Abbasid Empire and many traces allow us to evaluate a flourishing trade. Murwab, the largest settlement of this period is constituted of forty houses, one fort with two occupations and two mosques.
Abbasid Trade
From the beginning of the Abbasid Caliphate, the Caliph Al-Mansur built ex-nihilo the round city of Baghdad in 762 (Madinat as-Salam). This new capital on the banks of the Tigris River became the centre of power, transferring trade and culture from Damascus, the former capital of the Umayyads, to more eastern territories. It is in Baghdad that the oldest House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma), was founded in 832 during the reign of Al-Ma’mun (813-833). It is a particularly active establishment, specializing in the translation of works into Greek, Pehlevi and Syriac. Scientists, astronomers, mathematicians, thinkers and scholars came here from all over the Abbasid Empire, facilitating a constant interchange of ideas and goods into the Arab-Muslim world of that period.
